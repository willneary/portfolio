TPC Scottsdale’s final holes encourage Open drama, tight finishes

February 7, 2026

Originally published in The Arizona Republic

Boisterous fans. Beer showers. Joel Dahmen and Harry Higgs shirtless on the 16th green in 2022. Of all the things that distinguish the WM Phoenix Open from other tournaments on the PGA Tour, Scottie Scheffler has one to add.

The closing stretch.

“I think this golf course is one of the best ones we see all year in terms of an exciting finish,” said Scheffler, two-time winner of the WM Open and current World No. 1. “There is opportunity, but a lot of danger.”

TPC Scottsdale’s finishing holes encourage drama. With par 5s on holes 13 and 15, the stadium 16th par 3, the driveable par-4 17th hole, and the treacherous 18th, almost no lead is secure.

Scheffler knows the WM Open’s host course, TPC Scottsdale, as well as anyone. It’s where he secured his maiden PGA Tour victory in 2022, and where he defended that title in 2023.

In those years, he played the final six holes on Sunday in 4-under-par and 3-under-par, respectively. When he didn’t win, he was never able to surpass 1-under-par over that stretch.

Each hole presents its own unique balance of risk and opportunity. The 15th hole, a challenging par-5 with water left and an island green, is perhaps the best example.

“You look at the second shot on 15,” Scheffler said. “If you hit a really good shot there you can get a really good look for eagle, but if you hit a poor shot you’re going to be in the water staring bogey in the face.”

In four trips down the 15th hole in 2025, Scheffler went for the green on three occasions. The gamble paid off in the first round, as his 244-yard approach settled around 8 feet from the hole. The putt dropped for an eagle.

The final two rounds brought different results. Scheffler, on the edge of contention as he stood in the rough right of the 15th fairway, chose to go for the green. His shot drew left of the green and found water. He made bogey.

He’d find the water once again with his second shot the next day.

Others choose to air on the side of caution, rarely targeting the green with their second shot. Nick Taylor, champion of the 2024 WM Phoenix Open and runner-up in 2023, is one.

“If I’ve had 4-iron or less (into the green) I’ve laid up every time,” Taylor said.

He emphasized that this safer approach decreased the likelihood of scoring over par on the 15th hole while still allowing him an opportunity for a birdie with a wedge for his third shot.

But even this caution may not be rewarded.

“If you pull that second shot off (on the par-5 15th) you’ll have an eagle look,” said Sahith Theegala, now in his fifth full year on the PGA Tour. “But if not you would almost take a five. It’s such a hard wedge shot into those greens.”

The threat of water is not unique to the par-5 15th hole. It looms long and left of the green on the driveable-par-4 17th, and sits between the tee box and the fairway on hole 18.

These final two holes proved consequential for Theegala in 2022. Tied for the lead as he stood on the 17th tee, he opted to stay aggressive. His tee-ball, a 2-hybrid, kicked left, rolled over the left side of the green and found water.

He would finish one shot outside a playoff with a costly bogey on 17. Four years later he says he still finds himself thinking back to that one swing more often than he’d like.

The 16th hole, perhaps one of the most famous in golf, might not be as architecturally difficult as some others. But what it lacks in length and treachery it makes up for in atmosphere.

“If you just hit a poor shot (on the 16th hole) or get overwhelmed you can make a four there pretty easily,” Taylor said.

Staying calm in that coliseum is no small feat. The fans, famously rambunctious and often a few drinks in, won’t hesitate to boo a shot that sails wide of the green or settles in a bunker. But should a tee-shot find the bottom of the hole, showers of beer rain onto the players and the green.

On the par-3 16th and the holes that surround it the players are forced into decision. Be bold and risk disaster, or air towards safety and open the door for others? It’s a question that the Phoenix Open asks perhaps better than any other course down its final holes.

“I think it’s a fantastic finish that provides a great amount of opportunity,” Scheffler said, “but there can also be pretty severe penalties as well.”

As the final, leading groups work their way through this stretch under fading light on Sunday afternoon, this debate between aggression and caution will be on full display.

The WM Phoenix Open, more so than most tournaments on the PGA Tour, is not over until the final putt drops.

Caddies at the WM Phoenix Open face challenges at a unique atmosphere

February 8, 2026

Originally published in Golfweek

SCOTTSDALE, Ariz. ― The caddie’s job is not an easy one.

Every tournament asks a unique set of questions of caddies and their players. At the WM Phoenix Open, caddies must be aware of more quirks than usual.

Of course, there’s the invisible break on the 11th green that gently pulls putts towards the water. The subtle, swirling winds high above the 16th green. In many ways, these challenges are not dissimilar to those presented by courses across the PGA Tour.

But this tournament has a trick up its sleeve.

TPC Scottsdale’s design is but one variable to be accounted for. The Phoenix Open puzzle has an extra piece. The tournament’s fans and its well-renowned boisterous atmosphere warrant the caddies’ attention, and for first-timers, the loud, unforgiving and often inebriated crowds can be uncharted territory.

“We’re definitely kind of learning as we’re going,” said Greg Dyne, caddying for first-year PGA Tour member Neal Shipley. “But it’s fun. It’s a fun ride.”

And Dyne is lucky.

“The vibe is electric and (Shipley) kind of thrives on that,” he said. “It’s that one tournament a year where pretty much no rules apply, and we accept that coming in and you kind of embrace it.”

Max Bill, caddying for Phoenix Open debutant Marco Penge, previously worked on the DP World Tour. Like Penge, 2026 is Bill’s first foray into this tournament’s unparalleled energy.

“I have more confidence in (Penge) when it’s like this than when it’s flat calm and dead quiet,” Bill said.

The standard salary for PGA Tour caddies is a weekly stipend in addition to 10 percent of their player’s prize money for a first-place finish; roughly 6-8% for top-10 finishes and 5% for all other finishes. All are predicated on making the cut.

Shipley, shooting an up-and-down score of 1-under 71 on Friday, will play the weekend in his Phoenix Open debut.

Veteran caddie Joe Greiner, working now with young star Akshay Bhatia, made his debut at TPC Scottsdale with Kevin Chappell in 2016. His expertise has helped Bhatia settle near the top of the leaderboard this week, reaching 7-under-par through two rounds.

“I was really excited my first time here,” Greiner said, “but I treat it more like work. It’s just another tournament trying to do your job and not let anything affect you.”

It’s about managing the emotions of your player, Greiner explained, which comes with time and familiarity. The player’s energy can affect the shot. That level of comfort developed between player and caddie is huge.

“You know when they’re feeling really good and you don’t need to say anything,” Greiner said. “And you know when they might be a little anxious and you need to encourage them.”

TGL gives golfers first opportunity to represent their home cities

February 23, 2026

Originally published in The Arizona Republic

America’s most popular sports are almost always played by athletes who represent the nation’s biggest or most influential cities. Golf is the exception.

Golf is almost always played individually. Even when team events do exist, like the Ryder Cup, Presidents Cup and a select few events on the PGA Tour schedule, they still don’t assign players to teams by city like most other major sports.

TGL, a new golf league presented by TMRW Sports, is changing that.

This new venture, with its litany of technological innovations, also provides golfers a previously impossible opportunity: to play their sport at the professional level for their home city.

“It means the world to me,” said Sahith Theegala, a PGA Tour pro and Southern California native who plays in TGL for the Los Angeles Golf Club. “All my family is still in L.A. and it’s still home for me.”

TGL is an indoor league and relies on a 64-foot wide simulator screen for most shots and a rotating green (and bunker) complex for short game play.

The league features six teams representing cities across the U.S. A seventh team, the Motor City Golf Club of Detroit, will be the league’s first expansion team when it reaches its third season.

Theegala, who played at Pepperdine University, was so attached to L.A. that three days after the death of Lakers legend Kobe Bryant in a helicopter crash in January 2020, the then-senior wore his Bryant jersey over his golf shirt as he putted out for his fourth NCAA tournament victory.

Six years later, Theegala’s L.A. ties still run deep.

“I was just honored that they even chose me for the (L.A. Golf Club) team,” Theegala said, “and to play for my hometown is pretty epic.”

Another of the original six teams, the Boston Common Golf Ballfrogs, are represented by two New England natives, including Michael Thorbjornsen, raised in Wellesley, Massachusetts.

“Just being affiliated with an entity or a team now that is stationed in Boston or in Massachusetts is pretty cool,” Thorbjornsen said.

Thorbjornsen, just 24 years old and only recently added to the Ballfrogs’ roster as a designated alternate, proved pivotal in his team’s first victories in the young league.

Thorbjornsen arrived at TGL's home base, the SoFi Center in Palm Beach Gardens, Florida, sporting a Drake Maye jersey. Maye, the quarterback for the New England Patriots, was in the midst of building a case for MVP and leading his team to the playoffs at the time.

“There’s definitely a sense of pride in being a part of that team,” Thorbjornsen said. “A lot of people have a lot of pride in where they’re from and where they grew up, so having another team to back and have some passion for is great for everyone.”
